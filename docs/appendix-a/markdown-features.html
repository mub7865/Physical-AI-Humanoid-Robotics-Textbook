Safety Protocols: Working with Physical Robots ğŸ›¡ï¸

The First Law of Robotics: Do No Harmâ€‹

Unlike pure software, Physical AI has consequences. If you write a bad loop in Python, your computer freezes. If you write a bad loop in Robotics, your robot might drive off a table, overheat a motor, or injure someone.

This guide outlines the critical safety protocols you MUST follow when working with the hardware described in Chapter 8.

1. The Emergency Stop (E-Stop) ğŸ›‘â€‹

Every robot must have a "Kill Switch." This is a mechanism to cut power immediately if the robot behaves unpredictably.

CRITICAL SAFETY RULE

NEVER operate a mobile robot without a verified Software or Hardware E-Stop.

How to implement an E-Stop:â€‹

  • Software: Map a specific key (e.g., Spacebar or Enter) on your teleop keyboard to immediately publish 0 velocity to /cmd_vel.
  • Hardware: On larger robots like the Unitree Go2, always keep the remote controller in your hand with your finger on the "Lock/Stop" button.

2. Battery Safety (LiPo Handling) ğŸ”‹â€‹

Robots typically run on Lithium Polymer (LiPo) batteries. These are energy-dense but volatile.

Fire Hazard

LiPo batteries can catch fire if punctured, overcharged, or short-circuited. Never leave charging batteries unattended.

Best Practices:

  1. Storage: Store batteries in a fireproof LiPo bag.
  2. Voltage: Never discharge a cell below 3.2V. It will permanently damage the battery.
  3. Swelling: If a battery looks "puffy" or swollen, DISCARD IT IMMEDIATELY at a recycling center. Do not use it.

3. Mechanical Pinch Points ğŸ¤â€‹

Humanoid robots have powerful joints (knees, elbows) that close with significant force.

Watch Your Fingers

Keep hands and cables clear of joints while the robot is powered on. A servo motor does not "feel" pain; it will close on your finger if commanded to.

  • The Rule of Thumb: If the robot is powered, assume it can move at any time. AI agents are probabilistic; they might decide to move an arm unexpectedly.

4. Software Guardrails ğŸ’»â€‹

Before letting an LLM (like GPT-4) control a robot, you must implement a "Pre-Flight Check" in your code.

5. Sim-to-Real Checklist âœ…â€‹

Before you deploy code from Isaac Sim to the real Jetson Orin, go through this checklist:

  • Simulation Test: Did the robot survive 10/10 runs in Gazebo without crashing?
  • Speed Limits: Have you capped the maximum velocity (e.g., 0.5 m/s) in your config?
  • Space: Is the physical testing area clear of obstacles and pets?
  • Cables: Are all wires secured so they don't get caught in wheels/legs?

Final Thoughtâ€‹

"Move fast and break things" is a bad motto for robotics. "Move deliberately and verify everything" keeps your hardware (and fingers) intact.
